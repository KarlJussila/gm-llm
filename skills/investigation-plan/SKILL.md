# Investigation Design

You are designing the investigative spine of a session so the runner *executes* a discovery rather
than inventing one mid-scene. Use this whenever a session is meant to deliver a revelation — a
pattern the PC can piece together, a hidden truth, a conclusion that recontextualizes the mystery.

## Design each discovery

For every revelation the session should deliver, specify:

- **The conclusion** (DM-facing) — the realization on the far side of the investigation. Keep it off
  the player's screen; it's the target, not a beat to announce.
- **The pieces** — the concrete clues, observations, or data points that lead there, and where/how
  each surfaces in play.
- **How they combine** — the logic that turns the pieces into the conclusion. Make the pieces
  *complete enough* that the combination genuinely holds; don't leave a gap only you can bridge.
- **Who assembles it, and how** — pick the resolution mode:
  - **Player-assembled** — the pieces are laid out plainly and the player can connect them. The
    player does **not** have to *state* the conclusion; plan for them to act on it silently, later,
    or to grasp only its shape. That still counts.
  - **Character-assembled (check-gated)** — the synthesis leans on the character's expertise, which
    the player may not share. Name the check (Investigation, Arcana, Medicine, Insight…); on success
    the runner narrates what the *character* realizes. "What does my character see?" is the player
    invoking this — expect it, and make sure the plan tells the runner what the character concludes.
  - Many discoveries support both: lay the pieces out, and if the player doesn't bite, a check lets
    the character close the gap. Note which applies.
- **Knowledge flags** — mark each piece `(known)`, `(new reveal)`, or `(stays hidden)` against the
  ledger, and note which NPCs already know it (so they can react to it — see `npc-plan`).

## Check-gated reveals — the general rule

When information is gated behind a check (a data synthesis here, or a person's concealed tell in
`npc-plan`): the runner asks for the roll and narrates what the character perceives on success; a
low roll fails or yields a partial, ambiguous read. **Information is hard-won, never gifted** —
don't hand the conclusion over for free, and don't pre-narrate the gated detail before the roll. An
NPC's concealed background or deception has an extra rule on top of this — the *player* must initiate
the Insight check and the runner doesn't offer it; that lives in `npc-plan`.

## Principles

- **Cross-reference against canon.** Every clue, conclusion, and who-knows-what must be consistent
  with established campaign knowledge — the world/NPC/faction files, active arcs, recorded
  documents, and the PC knowledge ledger. Don't invent a fact that contradicts canon, don't place a
  clue an established secret rules out, and flag every reveal against the ledger.
- **Design the investigation; don't outsource it to the table.** Lay out the clues and the
  conclusion they support before play, including which checks gate which reveals. The runner
  improvises around how they land, but should never have to invent the investigative logic — or
  demand the player produce a conclusion the plan never built — on the fly.
- **Pieces complete enough to combine.** If the only path from clues to conclusion runs through your
  head, either add the missing piece or make it check-gated and let the character's expertise bridge
  it. Don't set partial pieces and then demand a clean takeaway.
- **Silence is a valid read.** Plan for the player to understand without verbalizing. They'll act on
  it when it's relevant or when later evidence confirms it.
